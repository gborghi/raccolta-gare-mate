*A family of equal mutually tangent circles aligned along a half-line; tangent line cuts the circles, chord lengths and a collinearity*

> A family of tangent circles.
> 
> In the plane, let $A$ be a point and $\Delta$ a half-line with origin $A$.
> 
> 1. We consider three circles $\Gamma_1$, $\Gamma_2$, $\Gamma_3$ of the same radius $r$, with centers respectively $O_1$, $O_2$, $O_3$, distinct and aligned in this order on the half-line $\Delta$. The circle $\Gamma_1$ passes through $A$ and the circle $\Gamma_2$ is tangent to $\Gamma_1$ and $\Gamma_3$. The diameters of the circles $\Gamma_1$, $\Gamma_2$, $\Gamma_3$ on the half-line $\Delta$ are denoted respectively $[AA_1]$, $[A_1A_2]$ and $[A_2A_3]$.
> 
> Through the point $A$, draw a line $(AD)$ tangent at $D$ to the circle $\Gamma_3$.
> 
>    a. Show that the line $(AD)$ cuts the circle $\Gamma_2$ at two distinct points $B$ and $C$. Calculate the length $BC$.
>    b. Show that the lines $(BA_1)$ and $(CA_2)$ are secant; let $P$ be their point of intersection. Show likewise that the lines $(CA_1)$ and $(BA_2)$ are secant; let $Q$ be their point of intersection. What can be said about the direction of the line $(PQ)$?
> 
> 2. More generally, we consider an integer $n$ strictly greater than $1$ and $n$ circles $\Gamma_1, \Gamma_2, \dots, \Gamma_n$ of the same radius $r$, with centers respectively $O_1, O_2, \dots, O_n$, distinct and aligned in this order on the half-line $\Delta$. The circle $\Gamma_1$ passes through $A$ and, for every $k$ with $1 \le k \le n-1$, the circle $\Gamma_{k+1}$ is tangent to the circle $\Gamma_k$. The diameters of the circles $\Gamma_1, \Gamma_2, \dots, \Gamma_n$ on the line $\Delta$ are denoted respectively $[AA_1], [A_1A_2], \dots, [A_{n-1}A_n]$.
> 
> Through the point $A$, draw a line $(AD)$ tangent at $D$ to the circle $\Gamma_n$. Show that, for every $k$ with $1 \le k \le n-1$, this line cuts the circle $\Gamma_k$ at two distinct points $B_k$ and $C_k$ (note that $B_1 = A$).
> 
>    a. Calculate the length $B_k C_k$ as a function of $n$, of $k$ and of $r$.
> 
> In the whole rest of the problem, one takes $r = 1$. One sets $L(n,k) = B_k C_k$.
> 
>    b. Show that, in order for $L(n,k)$ to be rational, it is necessary and sufficient that the following condition be satisfied:
>    $(C_1)$: there exists $a \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n(n-1) - k(k-1) = 4a^2$.
