*Deuxieme partie: equivalent conditions for a pseudo-rectangle triangle, rational/integer side triples, and related Diophantine equations.*

> Throughout this part, $a$, $b$, $c$ are strictly positive reals.
> 
> **1.** Let $(a,b,c)$ be a triple of strictly positive reals. Establish the equivalence of the following conditions:
>   i. there exists a triangle $ABC$ pseudo-rectangle at $A$ and obtuse at $B$ such that $AB = c$, $BC = a$ and $CA = b$;
>   ii. $b^2 - c^2 = a\sqrt{b^2 + c^2}$;
>   iii. there exist two reals $\rho > 0$ and $\theta$ with $0 < \theta < \dfrac{\pi}{4}$ such that $a = \rho\cos 2\theta$, $b = \rho\cos\theta$ and $c = \rho\sin\theta$.
> 
> These conditions being satisfied, show that $\theta$ measures one of the angles of the triangle $ABC$. How can one interpret $\rho$ geometrically?
> 
> **2.** Let $ABC$ be a triangle pseudo-rectangle at $A$, obtuse at $B$, whose side lengths are rational numbers; let $\rho$ and $\theta$ be the two reals defined in **1.iii.** In this question, one may use without justification the following trigonometric formulas, valid for every real $\varphi$ for which $\tan\varphi$ is defined:
> $$\cos 2\varphi = \frac{1 - \tan^2\varphi}{1 + \tan^2\varphi}, \qquad \sin 2\varphi = \frac{2\tan\varphi}{1 + \tan^2\varphi}.$$
>   a. Show that $\rho$ is rational, and deduce that $\tan\dfrac{\theta}{2}$ is rational. Let $p$ and $q$ be the strictly positive coprime integers such that $\tan\dfrac{\theta}{2} = \dfrac{p}{q}$.
>   b. Verify that $0 < p < q(\sqrt{2} - 1)$ and establish the existence of a strictly positive rational $r$ such that:
> $$a = r\,(p^4 - 6p^2q^2 + q^4)$$
> $$b = r\,(q^4 - p^4)$$
> $$c = 2\,p\,q\,r\,(p^2 + q^2).$$
> 
> **3.** Show conversely that the formulas of **2.b.** define the side lengths of a triangle pseudo-rectangle at $A$, obtuse at $B$, whose side lengths are rational.
> 
> **4.**
>   a. Let $p$ and $q$ be two strictly positive coprime integers. Determine the greatest common divisor of the three integers $p^4 - 6p^2q^2 + q^4$, $q^4 - p^4$, $2pq(p^2 + q^2)$ (one will discuss according to the parity of $p$ and $q$).
>   b. Describe the triples of integers $(a,b,c)$ such that there exists a triangle $ABC$, pseudo-rectangle at $A$, obtuse at $B$, such that $AB = c$, $BC = a$ and $CA = b$.
> 
> **5.** Solve in $\mathbb{N}^*$ the equation $x^2\,(y^2 + z^2) = (y^2 - z^2)^2$.
> 
> **6.** Solve in $\mathbb{Q}^*$ the equation $x^2\,(y^2 + z^2) = (y^2 - z^2)^2$.
> 
> **7.** Solve in $\mathbb{N}^*$ the equation $x^2\,(y^2 - z^2)^2 = (y^2 + z^2)^3$.
