*Single problem (Exercice 1) in four parts on 'trios' (a,b,c) with ab+bc+ca=0: geometric loci, integer/primitive trios and counting, a star-product of trios via complex numbers, and the arithmetic of integers represented by u^2+3v^2.*

> A \textbf{trio} is any triple of real numbers $(a,b,c)$, not all zero, satisfying the relation
> $$ab+bc+ca=0.$$
> When $a+b+c=1$, the trio $(a,b,c)$ is said to be \textbf{reduced}. Coordinates are referred to a direct orthonormal frame $\left(O\,;\,\vec{i},\,\vec{j},\,\vec{k}\right)$ of space.
> 
> \textbf{First part}
> Let $\mathcal{C}$ be the set of points with coordinates $(a,b,c)$ where $(a,b,c)$ is a trio. Let $\Gamma$ be the set of points with coordinates $(a,b,c)$ where $(a,b,c)$ is a reduced trio. Let $\mathcal{P}$ be the plane with equation $x+y+z=1$.
> 1. Do there exist trios $(a,b,c)$ such that $a+b+c=0$?
> 2. Show that $\mathcal{C}$ is a union of lines passing through $O$ and deprived of that point.
> 3. Show that $\Gamma$ is the intersection of a plane and a sphere of center $O$. What is the geometric nature of $\Gamma$?
> 4. Give the geometric nature of $\mathcal{C}$ and illustrate it with a sketch.
> 5. Let $L$ be a point fixed on $\Gamma$. Show that the volume $V$ of the tetrahedron $OLL'L''$, where $L'$ and $L''$ are two distinct points of $\Gamma$ different from $L$, is maximal when the edges issuing from $O$ are pairwise orthogonal, and then determine the coordinates of $L'$ and $L''$ as functions of those of $L$.
> 6. Show that the product $abc$ admits a maximum and a minimum as the point of coordinates $(a,b,c)$ ranges over $\Gamma$. State the reduced trios realizing these extrema.
> 
> \textbf{Second part}
> In this part and the following ones, a trio $(a,b,c)$ is said \textbf{rational} when $a$, $b$ and $c$ are rational numbers (elements of $\mathbb{Q}$); it is said \textbf{integer} when $a$, $b$ and $c$ are relative integers (elements of $\mathbb{Z}$); finally an integer trio is said \textbf{primitive} if $a$, $b$ and $c$ admit only $1$ and $-1$ as common divisors.
> 1. Determine the nature of the set $H_1$ of points of coordinates $(x,y,1)$ such that $(x,y,1)$ is a trio. Show that the point $\Omega_1$ of coordinates $(-1,-1,1)$ is a center of symmetry of $H_1$. What are the points of $H_1$ with integer coordinates?
> 2. For a given nonzero natural integer $h$, let $Z_h$ be the set of integer trios $(a,b,c)$ such that $c=h$. Determine $Z_h$ for $h=1$ and $h=2$.
> 3. Show that $Z_h$ is a finite set and express the number $N(h)$ of its elements as a function of the number of divisors of $h^2$ in $\mathbb{Z}$. Show that $4$ divides $N(h)-2$.
> 4. For every nonzero natural integer $h$, let $N'(h)$ be the number of integer trios $(a,b,c)$ such that at least one of the integers $a$, $b$ or $c$ is equal to $h$. Express $N'(h)$ as a function of $N(h)$ according to the parity of $h$.
> 5. Show that to every integer trio $(a,b,c)$ one can associate a triplet $(r,s,t)$ of integers such that $r$ and $s$ are coprime, $s$ is nonnegative, and such that $a=r(r+s)t$, $b=s(r+s)t$, $c=-rst$. State and prove a converse. For which trios $(a,b,c)$ is the triplet $(r,s,t)$ not unique?
> 6. Determine the triplets $(r,s,t)$ thus associated to the primitive trios. Deduce that if $(a,b,c)$ is a primitive trio, then $|abc|$, $|a+b|$, $|b+c|$ and $|c+a|$ are perfect squares of integers.
> 7. For every nonzero natural integer $h$, let $P(h)$ be the number of primitive trios $(a,b,c)$ such that $c=h$. Show that $P(h)$ is a power of $2$. For which integers $h$ does one have $P(h)=N(h)$? Make explicit a sequence of integers $(h_n)$ such that the sequence $\left(\dfrac{P(h_n)}{N(h_n)}\right)$ converges to zero.
> 8. Let $(a,b,1)$ be a trio. Show that there exist two sequences $(x_n)$ and $(y_n)$ converging respectively to $a$ and $b$ and such that for every $n$, $(x_n,y_n,1)$ is a rational trio.
> 9. Let $(a,b,c)$ be a reduced trio. Show that there exist three sequences $(x_n)$, $(y_n)$, $(z_n)$ converging respectively to $a$, $b$, $c$ and such that for every $n$, $(x_n,y_n,z_n)$ is a rational reduced trio.
> 
> \textbf{Third part}
> Let $j$ be the complex number $e^{\frac{2i\pi}{3}}$, that is $j=-\dfrac{1}{2}+i\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$. For every trio $T=(a,b,c)$, write $\widetilde{T}=(a,c,b)$, $S(T)=a+b+c$ and $z(T)=a+bj+cj^2$.
> 1. Compute the modulus of $z(T)$ as a function of $S(T)$. Can one have $z(T)=0$? Compute the cosine and the sine of an argument $\theta$ of $z(T)$ as functions of $a$, $b$ and $c$.
> 2. Let $z_0$ be a nonzero complex number. Determine the trios $T=(a,b,c)$ such that $z(T)=z_0$.
> 3. Given two trios $T_1$ and $T_2$, show that there exists a unique trio, denoted $T_1\ast T_2$, verifying $S(T_1\ast T_2)=S(T_1)\,S(T_2)$ and $z(T_1\ast T_2)=z(T_1)\,z(T_2)$. Compute $T_1\ast T_2$ as a function of $T_1$ and $T_2$. What can be said about an argument of $z(T_1\ast T_2)$? What can be said about an argument of $z(T_1\ast \widetilde{T_1})$?
> 4. If $T_1$ and $T_2$ are reduced, is $T_1\ast T_2$ reduced? If $T_1$ and $T_2$ are integer trios, is $T_1\ast T_2$ an integer trio? If $T_1$ and $T_2$ are primitive, is $T_1\ast T_2$ primitive?
> 5. Compare the trios $T_1\ast T_2$ and $T_2\ast T_1$; $(T_1\ast T_2)\ast T_3$ and $T_1\ast(T_2\ast T_3)$; $T_1$ and $T_1\ast(1,0,0)$.
> 6. Given two trios $T_1$ and $T_2$, solve the equation $T_1\ast T=T_2$ with the trio $T$ as unknown.
> 7. Let $T$ be a trio. Define a sequence of trios $(T_n)$ by $T_0=(1,0,0)$ and $T_{n+1}=T\ast T_n$. Compute $S(T_n)$. Given a natural integer $p$, solve the equation $T_p=T_0$ where the trio $T$ is the unknown.
> 
> \textbf{Fourth part}
> Let $A$ be the set of nonzero integers $m$ such that there exist two integers $u$, $v$ with $m=u^2+3v^2$.
> Let $A'$ be the set of complex numbers $z$ such that there exist two integers $u$, $v$ with $z=u+iv\sqrt{3}$ (one remarks that $|z|^2=u^2+3v^2$).
> Let $B$ be the set of nonzero integers $n$ such that there exist two integers $r$, $s$ with $n=r^2+rs+s^2$.
> 1. Show that the product of two elements of $A'$ belongs to $A'$, then that the product of two elements of $A$ belongs to $A$.
> 2. Show that if $p$ is a prime number that is an element of $A$, then $p=3$ or $4$ divides $p-1$.
> 3. Show that $A=B$ (one may notably remark that $r^2+rs+s^2=(r+s)^2-s(r+s)$).
> 4. Show that $4$ divides the elements $a$ of $A$ whose quotients belong to $A$, then that every odd element of $A$ [is a divisor of an element of $A$ multiplied by a power of $4$].
> (a) Let $m=u^2+3v^2$ be an odd integer such that the integers $u$ and $v$ are coprime and which admits a prime divisor $p$ not belonging to $A$. Show that there then exists a smallest strictly positive integer $n_0$ such that $n_0 p$ belongs to $A$. Show that $n_0$ is odd.
> (b) Establish the existence of two integers $u'$ and $v'$ of absolute value less than $\dfrac{p}{2}$ such that $p$ divides $u'-u$ and $v'-v$. Show that $p$ divides the nonzero integer $u'^2+3v'^2$ and that $n_0<p$.
> (c) Establish the existence of two nonzero coprime integers $u_0$ and $v_0$ such that $n_0 p=u_0^2+3v_0^2$.
> (d) Establish the existence of two integers $u_1$ and $v_1$ of absolute value less than $\dfrac{n_0}{2}$ such that $n_0$ divides $u_1-u_0$ and $v_1-v_0$. Show that $n_0$ divides the nonzero integer $u_1^2+3v_1^2$, which we denote $n_0 n_1$.
> (e) Deduce that such an integer $m$ cannot exist (one may consider the integer $n_0^2(n_0 p)$).
> 5. Show that every element of $A$ is written $m=C^2 p_1\cdots p_k$, where $C$ is a nonzero natural integer and the $p_i$ are distinct prime numbers that are elements of $A$.
> (a) Let $p$ be a prime number such that $3$ divides $p-1$ and let $K$ be the set of triplets $(x,y,z)$ where the integers $x$, $y$ and $z$ are strictly between $0$ and $p$, and such that $p$ divides $(xyz-1)$. Show that $K$ has $(p-1)^2$ elements, and that $3$ divides the number of elements of $K$ that do not satisfy $x=y=z$.
> (b) Deduce that there exists an integer $x$ strictly between $1$ and $p$ such that $p$ divides $x^2+x+1$, then that $p$ belongs to $A$. Describe the elements of $A$.
> 6. Let $D$ be the set of integers $d$ such that there exists an integer trio $(a,b,c)$ verifying $a+b+c=d$ and $abc\neq 0$. Show, thanks to question 5 of the second part, that every element of $D$ possesses a prime divisor that is an element of $A$. Conversely, what can be said about a nonzero integer admitting a prime divisor that is an element of $A$?
> 7. Deduce the elements of $D$ comprised, in the broad sense, between $2001$ and $2010$.
