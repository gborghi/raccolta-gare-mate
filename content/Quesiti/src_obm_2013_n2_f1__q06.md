*Incorrect multiples-of-7 count using digit-sum rule below 100*

> Student $D$ (we use this codename to protect his identity) did not pay attention in class, and when asked to check whether a number is a multiple of 7, he decided to use the digit-sum rule (the rule for 3): he sums all the digits and checks whether the result is a multiple of 7. For example, for 100: $1+0+0=1$, which is not a multiple of 7, so he correctly identifies 100 as not a multiple of 7. This incorrect method will indicate that a number is a multiple of 7 whenever the digit sum is a multiple of 7. How many positive integers less than 100 will this incorrect method \emph{incorrectly} indicate as multiples of 7 (i.e., the method says it is a multiple of 7 but it actually is not)?
> 
> (A) 0 \quad (B) 1 \quad (C) 2 \quad (D) 3 \quad (E) 4
