*Counting triangles on n points (no 3 collinear): maximal number t(n) under a shared-vertices condition, and minimal number s(n) under a chaining condition*

> Problem 2: Some geometric configurations.
> 
> In the plane, one considers $n$ points ($n \ge 3$) of which no three are ever collinear. One constructs all the triangles whose vertices are chosen among these $n$ points, and one requires the following condition:
> 
> Two distinct triangles, taken pairwise, have either zero or two vertices in common, but never exactly one.
> 
> Denote by $t(n)$ the maximal number of triangles one can thus form.
> 
> 1. Verify that $t(4) = 4$, then determine $t(5)$ and $t(6)$.
> 
> 2. Suppose $t(n) \le n$; deduce from this the value of $t(n)$ as a function of $n$.
> 
> One now considers the following condition on the triangles that can be formed:
> 
> Two distinct triangles that can be formed have a pair of vertices in common; in other words, one can pass from one triangle to the next.
> 
> Denote also by $s(n)$ the minimal number of triangles one can form respecting the preceding conditions (with the initial vertices).
> 
> 3. Verify that $s(4) = 3$, then determine $s(5)$ and $s(6)$.
> 
> 4. Determine $s(n)$ as a function of $n$.
>
