*Triangles ABC (called Cartesian) with integer sides and angle A = 2pi/3: rational lengths, incircle/excircle, and number-theoretic characterization*

> This problem deals with triangles $ABC$ said to be Cartesian, that is to say with integer sides $BC = a$, $CA = b$, $AB = c$ and angle at $A$ measuring $\dfrac{2\pi}{3}$ radians. Unless otherwise stated, $ABC$ is not assumed to be Cartesian.
> 
> 1. Denoting by $H$ its orthocenter, projected orthogonally at $(U, V, W)$ onto the three sides, determine which of the following are rational numbers: $AU$, $BV$, $CW$, $HA$, $HB$, $HC$, $HU$, $HV$, $HW$, $AW$, $AV$, $BU$, $BW$, $CV$ and $CU$.
> 2. Denoting by $I$ its incircle centre, $J$ the intersection of the interior bisector at $A$ with the exterior bisectors at the other vertices, and $P$, $Q$ the intersections of the line $BC$ with the two bisectors at $A$, determine which of the following are rational numbers: $PB$, $PC$, $QB$, $QC$, $AI$, $AJ$, $AP$ and $AQ$.
> 3. We now suppose $b$ and $c$ are coprime. Show that, up to exchanging $b$ and $c$, $a+b-c$ is a multiple of $3$ and $a-b+c$ is not.
> 4. We set $\dfrac{a+b-c}{3c} = \dfrac{p}{q}$ where $p$ and $q$ are strictly positive coprime integers. Denoting by $d$ the GCD of $p(3p+2q)$ and of $q(2p+q)$, calculate $a$, $b$, $c$ as a function of $p$, $q$ and $d$.
> 5. Show that $q$ is not a multiple of $3$, then that $d=1$.
> 6. Deduce a necessary and sufficient condition for a triangle to be Cartesian with coprime sides, then, by geometric remarks, an analogous characterization of triangles with coprime integer sides $BC = a$, $CA = b$ and $AB = c$ whose angle at $A$ measures $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$ radians.
