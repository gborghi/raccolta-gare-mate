*Study of the surface z^2 = x(x-1) - y(y-1): plane sections, axis of symmetry, nature of the surface, and rational/integer condition*

> Study of a surface.
> 
> The space is referred to an orthonormal frame $(O; \vec{\imath}, \vec{\jmath}, \vec{k})$. The coordinates (respectively the abscissa, the ordinate and the cote) of a point are denoted $x$, $y$ and $z$. We consider $\Sigma$ the set of points $M$ of coordinates $(x,y,z)$ such that:
> $$z^2 = x(x-1) - y(y-1).$$
> 
> 1. Let $\lambda$ be a real number and $P_\lambda$ the plane of equation $x = \lambda$. Show that the intersection of $\Sigma$ with $P_\lambda$ is a circle $C_\lambda$ whose center one determines, as a function of $\lambda$, the centre and the radius.
> 
> 2. Let $I$ be the point of coordinates $\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}, 0\right)$ and let $(d)$ be the line passing through $I$ with director vector $\vec{\imath}$. Show that the line $(d)$ is an axis of symmetry of $\Sigma$. Determine and draw the intersection of $\Sigma$ with the plane of equation $y = \frac{1}{2}$.
> 
> 3. Recognize the nature of the set $\Sigma$.
> 
> 4. Let $n$ be an integer strictly greater than $2$ and $k$ an integer such that $1 \le k \le n-1$. Show that $L(n,k)$ is rational if, and only if, the points of $\Sigma$ of abscissa $n$ and ordinate $k$ have an integer cote.
