*Study of the parabola $y=x^2/3-3/2$ and circles tangent to it: nature of the curve, the squared-distance function and its derivatives, characterising tangency by $g'_U(a)=0$, counting tangent circles, and bitangent circles.*

> We work in the Euclidean affine plane $\mathscr{P}$ with an orthonormal frame $(O,\vec{i},\vec{j})$. Let $S$ be the curve of equation:
> $$y=\frac{x^2}{3}-\frac{3}{2}.$$
> 
> 1. What is the geometric nature of $S$?
> 
> 2. For every pair $(u,v)$ of real numbers we denote by $U$ the point of coordinates $(u,v)$, and for $x$ in $\mathbb{R}$ we denote by $M(x)$ the point of $S$ with abscissa $x$. We set:
> $$f_U(x)=UM(x),\qquad g_U(x)=\left[f_U(x)\right]^2$$
>   a. Compute $g_U$, $g'_U$ and $g''_U$. Solve the equation $g''_U(x)=0$.
>   b. Give the table of variations of $f_U$ (one does not seek to compute explicitly the real numbers $x$ for which $f_U$ admits a relative extremum).
> 
> 3. We say that a circle $C$ with centre $U$ and radius $UM$ is tangent at $M$ to $S$ if $M$ is a point of $S$ and the tangents at $M$ to $C$ and to $S$ coincide. Let $U$ be a point of the plane not belonging to $S$, and let $a$ be in $\mathbb{R}$. Show that the circle with centre $U$ and radius $UM(a)$ is tangent at $M(a)$ to $S$ if and only if $g'_U(a)=0$.
> 
> 4.
>   a. Show that every point $U$ not belonging to $S$ is the centre of at least one and at most 3 circles tangent to $S$.
>   b. For $U$ not belonging to $S$, we denote by $n(U)$ the number of reals $x$ for which the circle with centre $U$ and radius $UM(x)$ is tangent at $M(x)$ to $S$. For $1\le i\le 3$, characterise by an equality or a simple inequality the set of points $U$ not belonging to $S$ such that $n(U)=i$. One may be led to discuss according to the sign of $81u^2-16v^3$. Make a sketch representing $S$ and the sets found.
> 
> 5.
>   a. Let $a$ be in $\mathbb{R}$. We denote by $D(a)$ the tangent at $M(a)$ to $S$. Give an equation of $D(a)$.
>   b. We denote again by $U$ the point of $\mathscr{P}$ with coordinates $(u,v)$. Discuss, as a function of $u$ and $v$, the number of solutions $a$ of the equation $U\in D(a)$.
>   c. Suppose that the equation $U\in D(a)$ admits two distinct solutions $a_1$ and $a_2$. Show that if $UM(a_1)=UM(a_2)$, then $u=0$.
>   d. Suppose $U\in\mathscr{P}$. Now assume that there exists a circle with centre $U$ tangent to $S$ at two distinct points $M$ and $N$ of $S$. Show that the tangents to $S$ at $M$ and $N$ are concurrent, and that if we denote by $V$ their point of intersection then $VM=VN$.
>   e. Determine the set of points $U$ not belonging to $S$ for which there exists a circle with centre $U$ tangent to $S$ at two distinct points of $S$.
