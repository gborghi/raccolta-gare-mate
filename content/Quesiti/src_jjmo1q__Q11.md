# Japan Junior Mathematical Olympiad 2003 — Quesito 11

*Fill-in-the-blank: operations on integer pairs, achievable products, and extremal values*

> Fill each box $\boxed{\phantom{X}}$ with the appropriate number.
> 
> Consider a pair $(x, y)$ where $x$ and $y$ are positive integers. We consider two operations:
> 
> **(A)** Choose a positive integer $a$ with $a \le y$; replace $(x, y)$ by $\left(ax,\, \dfrac{y}{a}\right)$. (This requires $a \mid y$.)
> 
> **(B)** Replace $(x, y)$ by $(x + 1,\, y + 1)$.
> 
> Starting from $(1, 1)$, apply these two operations repeatedly. For example: applying (B) gives $(2, 2)$; applying (A) with $a = \tfrac{3}{2}$... the example continues showing that from $(1,1)$ applying (B) gives $(2,2)$, then (A) with $a = \tfrac{3}{2}$ gives $\left(3, \tfrac{4}{3}\right)$... 
> 
> *(Note: operation (A) does not change the product $xy$; operation (B) changes it.)*
> 
> The problem asks to determine, among all pairs reachable from $(1,1)$ by these operations, the maximum product $xy$ that is a perfect square $k^2$, subject to the constraint that operation (B) is used at most $3$ times in total. Fill in the blanks:
> 
> 1. Applying (A) does not change $xy$; after one application of (B) the product increases. If before applying (B) the pair has product $xy = k^2$, the new product is at most $\boxed{\phantom{XX}}$ and at least $\boxed{\phantom{XX}}$.
> 2. For $k \ge 1$, the number of pairs $(x, y)$ with $xy = k^2$ reachable from $(1,1)$ is at most $\boxed{\phantom{XX}}$.
> 3. When (B) is applied exactly $\mathbf{3}$ times, the maximum product $xy$ (which must be a perfect square) is $\boxed{\phantom{XX}}$ if the maximum is taken over all such sequences. The two pairs achieving the two largest perfect-square products are $\big(\boxed{\phantom{X}},\, \boxed{\phantom{X}}\big)$ and $\big(\boxed{\phantom{X}},\, \boxed{\phantom{X}}\big)$, and these cannot be achieved with fewer than $3$ applications of (B).
