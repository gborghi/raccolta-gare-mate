*Exercise 2, Q1: prime-counting pi(n), initial values*

> Exercise 2 (general track following the maths specialty) - Super primes. We recall that a prime number is a non-zero natural integer having exactly two distinct positive divisors: $1$ and itself. Thus $1$ is not prime since it has only $1$ divisor. We recall also that there exist infinitely many prime numbers, which one can enumerate; the smallest prime numbers are $2$, denoted $p_1$; then $3$, denoted $p_2$, and so on. One thus denotes $p_n$ the $n$-th prime number. One gives, by way of illustration, the ordered list of the first fifteen prime numbers: $p_1 = 2$; $p_2 = 3$; $p_3 = 5$; $p_4 = 7$; $p_5 = 11$; $p_6 = 13$; $p_7 = 17$; $p_8 = 19$; $p_9 = 23$; $p_{10} = 29$; $p_{11} = 31$; $p_{12} = 37$; $p_{13} = 41$; $p_{14} = 43$; $p_{15} = 47$. For every natural integer $n$, one denotes $\pi(n)$ the number of prime numbers less than or equal to $n$. One notes that this notation, $\pi(n)$ or $\pi_n$, is usual and clear in context, but has nothing to do with the $\pi$ of plane geometry. Study of the sequence $(\pi_n)_{n \ge 0}$. (1) Justify that $\pi_0 = 0$ and $\pi_3 = 3$. Compute $\pi_1$, $\pi_2$, $\pi_6$, $\pi_{47}$ and $\pi_{49}$.
