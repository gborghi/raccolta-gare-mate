*Count arrangements of 2n objects in pairs, order immaterial*

> Prove that the number of ways of arranging $2n$ distinguishable objects in $n$ pairs is
> $$1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2n-1)$$
> if the order of the pairs and the order of the objects within each pair are both immaterial. For $4$ objects $a, b, c, d$ the $3$ pairings are $ab, cd$; $ac, bd$; $ad, bc$.
> 
> A party of $10$ people consisting of $5$ married couples is to be split into $5$ pairs. A pair may consist of two men, two women or a man and a woman, but must not be a married couple. In how many ways can this arrangement be made if order, as above, is immaterial? Explain your reasoning carefully.
